---
title: Pokopium, or How the State of the World Brought Me Right Back to Nintendo
date: 2026-03-13
tags: Pokemon, games, reviews, blabbing
---

So you might be wondering how the US going to war got me to play a game on a console that I didn't even think I would purchase, but honestly I think I want to ask you- aside from the economy, possibly crippling depression, hopelessness when faced with the state of the world, and a bank account geared more towards necessities; what is stopping YOU from playing this fuckass game.

Which, honestly, valid.

I'm lucky enough that I'm in a place where I can buy a Switch 2, and the game, and for that I'm lucky. I woke up a few days ago, did a scan of the news (I honestly can't read it in detail as much as I would like to, or I do, quite literally, get depressed) and saw the headlines that the US had bombed Iran. I moved to this country about a decade ago, if I had a nickel for every time I saw a president attack the middle east, crash the economy, and blame it on minorities I guess I'd have three nickels, which isn't a lot- but it's weird that it's happened THRICE.

Then my wife and I knew what to do ASAP. We started with household stuff, made sure any tank requiring oil or gas was refilled, called the oil company (our heating runs on oil) to refill, and figured out if there's anything else we want to stock before prices skyrocket. We were lucky enough to have my PC break down JUST before the RAM prices decided to jump the shark, so we had already gotten essentially new computers (ours were 6 years old). And in the midst of this chaos what little social media I have began to be inundated with one game: Pokopia.

When the Switch first came out I was disappointed, I think not having an OLED screen in 2026 is stupid, I think it's a bit too expensive, and honestly I'd rather buy a nice Android handheld. But then, Pokopia. One of my friends, Vincent, began to message me and almost immediately I realized I kind of wanted it. Combine that with the fact that I had been considering if my wife and I would get a Switch before RAM prices, tariffs, and literal war increased the already expensive price tag and suddenly the Switch 2 wasn't looking so bad.

Playing Pokopia ended up being probably the best coping mechanism I could have asked for; it felt just like Animal Crossing did during the pandemic. I could end my day, stop looking at the bad screen and look at the good screen. So how about gameplay? Any good?

I'll admit the beginning felt a bit overwhelming- I explored the map and the prospect of having to water every corner of the wasteland felt like waaay too much work. Fortunately the game limits you to a small section of the map while you learn mechanics, and during that time I found what would hook me: the Pokémon's personalities.

I've always been a Squirtle girlie, I'll admit it. But something about Bulbasaur being dumb as rocks immediately endeared me to them. I began to run around with the leafy idiot restoring habitats and meeting Pokémon and it became clear that this aspect would be what distinguished Pokopia from something like Animal Crossing.

In Animal Crossing the villagers' dialogue felt so recycled, once you had a specific villager type in your town, you could guess what they would tell you on any given day. While I'm sure eventually the dialogue may become a bit repetitive, the truth is that each Pokémon is dripping with charm and personality in a way that I WISH AC: New Horizons had, in a way that makes me nostalgic for the older Animal Crossing games, in a way that makes me certain I'll play this game and be motivated even through more tedious mechanics. Mechanics which, luckily, are already opening up.

Each ability Ditto has feels well thought out, the game gives them to you gradually, some even being optional, so each improvement inspires you to go back and re-explore older areas with new abilities. You begin in a map that feels a bit like a basin, high walls cutting you off, only to learn with each new unlocked mechanic new verticality to the map, underground areas, and fun ways to reshape the land and help your Pokémon feel at home after whatever disaster has happened.

Speaking of disasters, the story centers around ecological disasters ravaging the world of Pokémon. Humans have left the earth and left their Pokémon… behind? In storage? Perhaps I'm not far enough into the story, or this is left intentionally vague, but humans did not take their Pokémon with them. My own headcanon is that the PC systems storing Pokémon are failing, leading to them appearing in places where the system deems is a suitable habitat. This is why often they'll say they were just napping when they ended up in your habitat.

This legitimately tragic backdrop, further expanded upon in notes and pictures and messages you find from humans, is such a good contrast to the bouncy, bright, and adorable gameplay. The game beautifully balances the longing and trauma these Pokémon have experienced with their happiness and hope that perhaps, if you fix enough of the environment, their trainers might return…

When Pokopia released I honestly thought it would be a game I skipped until later, but trying to find pockets of peace in a world full of turmoil, trying to build spaces to feel hopeful in when the world feels cold and dark, it's not just something I want to do in game. Oftentimes for me games feel like an escape, a place I can go to and feel free from my own anxieties and fears and reclaim some peace and joy. It feels honestly beautiful, I think, to be able to share that with some of the creatures that were so pivotal to my imagination as a child. So both as Ditto, and as myself, I'll be using Pokopia to rebuild a bit of my own peace, block by block, and day by day.
